His Works

Whereabouts

Dr. Jose Rizal's remarkable journey across continents, from his birthplace in Calamba, Laguna, to the halls of European academia, reflecting his lifelong quest for knowledge and advocacy for Filipino emancipation.

Philippines

(1861-1882): Born in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines, Rizal spent his formative years in his homeland before embarking on his journey abroad.

Spain

In 1882-1890, Rizal studied medicine at Universidad Central de Madrid, Spain, where exposure to Enlightenment ideals ignited his nationalist passion.

Germany

In 1887, Rizal traveled to Heidelberg, Germany, to specialize in ophthalmology. There, he immersed himself in academia and culture, expanding his intellectual horizons.

European Countries

In 1888, Rizal toured Europe, including France, Belgium, and Austria-Hungary, gathering insights that shaped his activism and writings.

England

In 1889, Rizal lived in London, writing "Noli Me Tangere" to expose Spanish colonial injustices, engaging in fervent literary and political activities.

Philippines

In 1890, Rizal briefly returned to the Philippines before his 1892 exile to Dapitan. He persisted in advocating for social reforms and improved education and health.

Dapitan

In Dapitan 1892-1896, Rizal founded a school, practiced medicine, and pursued scientific interests, staying connected with fellow reformists through correspondence.

Philippines

Exiled, returned, arrested, executed: Rizal, a martyr for Philippine independence, met his fate in Manila's Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park) on December 30, 1896.

Rizal's Work

Noli Me Tangere

(Touch Me Not)

Published in 1887, this novel is considered one of the most influential works in Philippine literature. It exposes the injustices and abuses of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines, particularly the corruption and oppression perpetrated by the friars and colonial officials.

El Filibusterismo

(The Reign of Greed)

Published in 1891 as a sequel to "Noli Me Tangere," this novel delves deeper into the themes of social inequality, corruption, and rebellion. It depicts the continuing struggle against Spanish oppression and the consequences of colonialism on Filipino society.

Mi Último Adiós

(My Last Farewell)

Written on the eve of his execution, this poem serves as Rizal's final farewell to his beloved homeland, expressing his love for the Philippines and his desire for freedom and justice.

Filipinas Dentro de Cien Años

(The Philippines Within a Century)

This essay, written in 1889, envisions the future of the Philippines and predicts the country's eventual independence from colonial rule. It reflects Rizal's optimism and belief in the Filipino people's capacity for self-governance.

Letter to the Women of Malolos

Addressed to the women of Malolos, Bulacan, this letter was written in 1889 in response to their request for permission to establish a night school for women. In it, Rizal champions women's rights and education, emphasizing the importance of women's empowerment in society.

About Rizal

Dr. Jose Rizal

Jose Rizal, born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, was a renowned Filipino nationalist, scholar, and martyr, pivotal in the fight against Spanish colonization. His education and travels in Spain and Germany shaped his advocacy for justice and Filipino identity. Through his novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," he exposed colonial abuses, showcasing his multifaceted talent as a writer, artist, scientist, and linguist. Despite persecution, his dedication to reform persisted, leading to exile in Dapitan. However, his return to Manila marked his involvement in the Philippine Revolution, tragically culminating in his arrest and execution in 1896. Rizal's sacrifice ignited a fervor for independence and cemented his status as a national hero, inspiring generations of Filipinos to pursue freedom and equality.
